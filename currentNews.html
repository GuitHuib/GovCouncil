Executive clemency for offenses violating state law refers to both pardons and commutations and is a power held in Massachusetts by the governor, with the advice and consent of the Massachusetts Governor's Council.

To properly understand the role of the Parole Board, one needs to understand the difference between pardons and commutations and how parole works. A pardon is forgiveness of the offense. A commutation is not forgiveness of a crime but a reduction in sentence. An incarcerated person becomes eligible for parole, i.e. supervised release into the community, after they have served the minimum number of years of a sentence that is a range for example, 15 years to life. The person appears before the Parole Board which determines whether an individual continues to be a risk to public safety or not. [Add very brief 1-2 sentence explanations of each]

Pardons

Clemency in the form of a pardon functions as forgiveness of an offense to “remove the barriers that are sometimes associated with a criminal record, thereby facilitating the reintegration of the petitioner into his or her community.”

Petitions for pardons are filed with the Advisory Board of Pardons (aka the Parole Board). The Board reviews and decides whether to grant a hearing. After the hearing, the Board votes on whether to recommend that the governor pardon the individual. If the Governor recommends a pardon, the Governor’s Council must confirm with a majority of positive votes. The lieutenant governor casts the deciding vote if there is a tie.

Governor Baker issued 17 pardons in the fall of 2022, mostly for decades-old offenses. All were unanimously confirmed by the Governor’s Council except for two, which Governor Baker withdrew when it appeared he did not have the votes on the Council after a tumultuous hearing with strong opposition.

In contrast, Governor Tom Wolf of Pennsylvania, a Democrat who came into office the same time as Baker, pardoned 1,906 formerly incarcerated people and commuted 45 life sentences, as of April 2022.

Commutations

Typically, commutation petitions are filed by people serving 1 st degree murder and thus life without parole sentences. A commutation reduces such a sentence to life with the possibility of parole after at least 15 years.

Petitions for commutations are filed with the Advisory Board of Pardons (aka the Parole Board). And similar to the pardon process, the Board reviews and decides whether to grant a hearing. After the hearing, the Board votes on whether to recommend commutation to the governor.

If the governor grants the commutation request, it is sent to the Governor’s Council to confirm. The Council holds its own public hearing. A majority of votes is needed to ratify the governor’s decision. If there is a tie vote, the lieutenant governor casts the deciding vote.

If confirmed, the petitioner is now eligible for parole and returns to the Parole Board for a hearing.

The Parole Board

The Governor’s Council provides advice and consent to gubernatorial appointments to the Parole Board, voting on appointments after a public hearing .

In addition to pardons and commutations, and parole hearings for people serving life sentences with the possibility of parole, board members conduct parole hearings for all people serving less than life sentences. Board members are also responsible for termination of parole petitions from those serving parole. The agency is also charged with: "providing notice and assistance to victims, supervising people on parole in the community, and providing reentry services to those leaving custody with no mandated post-release supervision.”

Hearings for people serving life sentences with the possibility of parole are held on Tuesdays and Thursdays in Natick at the Parole Agency headquarters. They are public but there is no live streaming. All other parole hearings ,i.e., people serving less than life sentences, are held the other days of the week on site at the prisons, by one or two parole board members. These hearings are not public and no counsel is permitted. Decisions are made on the spot.

Governor Baker made nine nominations to the Board during his tenure. Three received a unanimous vote. The other six hearings featured significant opposition based primarily on what was perceived of as a lack of expertise in mental health and substance abuse or a preponderance of prosecutorial background.
